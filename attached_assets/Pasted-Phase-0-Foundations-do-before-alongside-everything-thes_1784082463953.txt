Phase 0 — Foundations (do before/alongside everything; these are cheap now and brutal to retrofit)
1. Agent-turn architecture refactor. Evolve "one LLM call per inbound message" to "one agent turn per debounced batch," where a turn may include tool calls. Keep the fast single-call path for chitchat. This unblocks real lookups (#4), debouncing (#5), and every proactive feature.
2. The Plan entity. A plans table (title, date/time, venue, attendees, status: proposed → deciding → confirmed → done) that polls, bookings, reminders, feedback, and memory all hang off. The anchor object for the whole product.
3. Preference privacy model. public/private flags on profile fields, plus the hard rule: private fields shape recommendations but never appear in group-visible text. One "since Bob's on a budget..." leak ends trust permanently.
4. Proactive-messaging budget. A per-user/per-thread frequency governor with priority ranking (occasion reminders > plan reminders > nudges > serendipity). Every proactive feature below draws from this budget. Blocking the number is unrecoverable churn.
5. Feedback/outcomes table. Ratings, poll winners, suggestions picked vs. ignored. The input to the taste engine and your proof that "continuously learning" is a mechanism, not a claim.
Phase 1 — MVP core loop (the cut line: ship when the full loop works end-to-end)
6. Real venue/activity recommendations. Google Places (or Yelp Fusion) as a tool call so suggestions are real venues with hours, price, and links. The existential feature — a concierge that can't look anything up isn't one.
7. Group-chat etiquette. Respond only when addressed or on clear planning intent; debounce rapid-fire messages into one turn; per-thread mute. Include passive-learning disclosure at onboarding ("I pay attention even when I'm quiet, so I can plan better") and an honest "what do you know about me?" command with correction/deletion. The #1 churn risk, handled.
8. Date/time coordination. Scheduling polls ("which nights work: Thu / Fri / Sat") with intersection logic — Doodle inside iMessage, reusing your existing poll machinery with a type: date flag. The harder half of group planning.
9. Proactive nudges + reminders (scheduler). pg-boss/BullMQ jobs: nudge non-voters, "dinner is tomorrow at 7," revive stalled plans. Your retention engine, governed by the messaging budget.
10. Calendar output. .ics attachment or Google Calendar link when a plan confirms. Trivially cheap, high perceived polish, plants the plan in the user's life outside the thread.
11. Post-plan feedback loop. Next-day "How was Lucali?" One-word replies feed the outcomes table, recommendations visibly sharpen, and it doubles as a natural re-engagement touchpoint. This closes the learning loop the pitch promises.
Phase 2 — Lovability & growth (fast follows; make it sticky and shareable)
12. Booking deep links. After approval, send the Resy/OpenTable link with party size/time pre-filled. Honest, useful, zero partnership dependency; your provider columns await the real integration later.
13. Group invite + member onboarding flow. Deliberate design for how the bot enters a group and how un-onboarded members get a one-line in-thread intro plus optional 1:1 DM — never a questionnaire in the group. Every group member is a prospective user; this is the growth loop.
14. The tiebreaker persona. On stall detection, the agent makes a confident call: "Executive decision: Thai, 7:30. Object within the hour or it's locked." A blame-free decision-maker is a real social function, and it's mostly prompt engineering. Highest magic-per-effort on the list.
15. Occasion memory. Capture birthdays, anniversaries, "when Jake visits in March" from passing mentions; surface them two weeks out with an offer to plan. Remembering what people themselves forget — nearly free given profile-updates + the scheduler.
16. Plan cards. A clean visual card (venue, time, who's in, map thumbnail) sent via Sendblue when plans confirm. Screenshot-able, forwardable — and forwarding a plan card is a referral.
17. Texting UX polish (continuous). Typing indicators during tool calls, splitting long replies into natural bubbles, tapbacks counting as votes. Sprinkled in throughout, since "feels like texting a person" is the premise.
Phase 3 — The compounding moat
18. The group taste engine. The centerpiece, built incrementally from Phase 0 onward, with three behaviors: (a) constraint satisfaction without disclosure — proposals quietly satisfy everyone's constraints, never explaining whose constraint drove what; (b) private aggregation over DM — sensitive inputs collected 1:1, only aggregates surface to the group, a capability no human organizer has; (c) cross-thread memory — preferences follow the person by phone number, so the concierge already knows half the members of any new group it joins. That "wait, it remembers me?" moment is the story people retell.
19. Proactive serendipity (rationed). Weather + plan history + group cadence: "75° Saturday and you three haven't met up in six weeks — rooftop?" The screenshot-and-share moment, strictly rationed by the messaging budget because it's also the fastest path to creepy.
