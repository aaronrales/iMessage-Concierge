(1) Gaps against what we aligned:
The corpus is empty — and that's the whole moat. YELP_API_KEY isn't set, so the population job has never run, meaning every recommendation today falls through to the raw Yelp fallback: the exact commodity-data outcome the BRD exists to prevent. The single highest-leverage next step isn't code — it's the operational pass: set the key, run population neighborhood-by-neighborhood, and work the /venues review queue to human-approve the first Tier 1 set. Everything downstream is waiting on this.
The group taste engine and the corpus aren't talking to each other. This is the one real code gap. buildGroupConstraintSummary feeds constraints to the prompt, and the corpus has dietary_breadth, group_friendliness, and price attributes — but lookupCorpusVenues never receives the group's constraints, so constraint satisfaction relies entirely on the LLM reading a text summary and hoping the right venues surfaced. Wiring group constraints into corpus ranking (filter/boost by dietary breadth vs. the group's needs, group-friendliness vs. party size, price vs. budgets) is the connective tissue between your two biggest investments, and it's what makes the "quietly satisfies everyone" magic reliable rather than probabilistic.
Smaller items: (a) replit.md is significantly stale — the architecture section still describes the old single-call, no-tools design, which will actively mislead the next dev session; worth a refresh pass now. (b) The ignored recommendation outcome exists in the enum but nothing logs it — a cheap sweep marking shown-but-never-picked events as ignored when a plan resolves completes the Path 3 dataset. (c) Calendar output is a Google link only; for an iMessage product, an .ics(Apple Calendar) option is arguably the more native choice. (d) scoreFreeText treats "quiet" as universally positive and "loud" as negative — that conflates attribute polarity with quality (a loud room is good for a rowdy birthday); fine for v1, but flag it for the scoring iteration. (e) The planning-intent regex will miss common triggers — "drinks?", "coffee?", "who's around sat?" — and silence reads as "the bot ignored us"; consider a broader lexicon or a tiny LLM intent check on borderline messages. (f) Zero tests, yet the product's correctness now lives in a dozen regex/deterministic modules (etiquette, budget, scrubber, poll matching, tapbacks) that will regress silently — these are the cheapest possible tests to write. (g) Timezones are implicitly UTC everywhere; fine for NYC-only, but make the America/New_York assumption explicit before a Chicago group finds their reminder at 4am.
(2) New magic, grounded in what the product has become:
The pattern I'd exploit: you built rich infrastructure (weather, corpus attributes, private DM rails, plan history, card rendering) that's each used for exactly one thing. The cheapest magic is second uses.
Weather-contingent plan rescue. A confirmed plan at a patio spot + the forecast turning 24h out → proactive: "Rain Saturday — want me to move you inside? Two similar spots nearby have tables." You already have weather.ts, the scheduler, plan venues, and the outdoor_seating attribute; this is a join, not a feature. It's the moment the concierge stops being a planner and becomes a guardian of the plan — nobody forgets the assistant that saved their birthday dinner.
The day-of logistics bundle. Upgrade the plan reminder from "dinner at 7" to genuinely useful intel drawn from corpus attributes you already extracted: "Heads up — walk-in only, Friday waits run ~45 min, but the bar's great for waiting." reservation_reality and bar_while_waiting were built to help choose venues; reusing them day-of doubles their value for zero new data cost.
Traditions. Detect cadence in plan history — three Thursday dinners running — and offer: "Should I just make this a standing thing? I'll find a spot each week." Recurring plans are the retention endgame: the concierge stops waiting to be summoned and becomes part of the group's rhythm. Modest schema addition (recurrence on plans) plus a scheduler job.
Instant group creation. Right now activation requires someone manually adding the number to a thread. If Sendblue's group-creation API allows it, "start a group with Amy and Jake for Saturday" from a 1:1 → the concierge spins up the thread and intros itself. That collapses your biggest activation friction into one text.
